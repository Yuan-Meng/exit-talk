optimal strategy

expected information gain (EIG)

\mathrm{I}(x_i) =-\log_2 {\mathrm{P}(x_i)} = -\log_2 \frac{1}{2} = 1

1. surprisal of each hypothesis

2. average surprisal = information entropy (shannon, 1948)

\mathrm{H}(X) = \sum_{i}^{n}\mathrm{P}(x_i)\mathrm{I}(x_i) = -\sum_{i}^{n}\mathrm{P}(x_i)\log \mathrm{P}(x_i) = 1

3. reduction in entropy after intervention (e.g., turning on green) and outcome = information gain

\mathrm{IG}(a) = \mathrm{H}(X) - \mathrm{H}(X|a,y) = 1 - 0 = 1

expected information gain over all outcomes 👉 guides intervention

don't know what's gonna happen!

\mathrm{EIG}(a) = \mathrm{H}(X) - \sum_{j}^{m}\mathrm{P}(y_i|a)\mathrm{H}(X|a,y_j) = 1

suboptimal strategy

positive test strategy (PTS)

\mathrm{PTS}(a) = \max_{g}[\frac{DescendantLinks{a,g}}{TotalLinks_g}]

test chosen hypothesis efficiently

  1. begin with one structure 
  2. score each intervention by  the proportion of links it can affect
  3. repeat for the other structure

infer weight of EIG

V(a) = \theta \cdot \mathrm{EIG}(a) + (1 - \theta) \cdot \mathrm{PTS}(a)

1. each person's weight of EIG: θ

2. value of an intervention

3. probability choosing intervention

\mathrm{P}(a) = \frac{\exp(V(a)/\tau)}{\sum_{a \in A}\exp(V(a)/\tau)}
  • τ 👉 ∞: maximize V(a)
  • τ 👉 ∞: choose randomly

key findings

  • study 1: unlike adults who mainly used EIG, 5- to 7-year-olds mostly relied on PTS to select interventions  
  • study 2-3: only asking children (5- to 11-year-olds) to explain intervention choices didn't change their strategies
  • study 3: EIG-based explanation training led 9- to 11-year-olds to rely more on EIG, but not younger children

explain the "inexplicable" 

  • why use PTS at all?
    • not wrong if testing one hypothesis at a time
    • scales more easily than EIG 
    • test causal stability (lapidow & walker, 2020, 2021), since stable causal relations are "better"? (blanchard et al., 2018; vasilyeva et al., 2018)
    • different types of PTS: make everything or the same thing happen?

"yellow is most useful because it can turn on every single one."

"red always goes to green, so it helps in both examples."

explain the "inexplicable" 

  • why was explaining intervention choices alone not enough to change intervention strategies? 
    • explain an outcome (e.g., why car wouldn't start): wrong explanations still pertain to car-related features
    • explain an intervention (e.g., why check the battery): if wrong, often not in the ballpark 👉 recognize current uncertainty, simulate all possible interventions + outcomes, and "re-compute" new uncertainty following each intervention-outcome pair...
  • why did training only help older but not younger kids?
    • 9- to 11-year-olds: adopted EIG-based explanations throughout the experiment
    • 7- to 8-year-olds: reverted back to PTS-based explanations after training

to learn from thinking, still need to think about the right thing, albeit not necessarily in the right way?

even explicitly training on difference-making requires certain level of preparedness (e.g., counterfactual reasoning, working memory...)  

future directions

  • control (n ≈ 10 left): "if alex turns on the {yellow, green, red} light bulb, what would happen in each picture?" 
  • third-party judgment: can children tell if someone else's intervention is useful? does it predict their own strategy? 
  • who benefits from explaining: measure children's baseline strategies 👉 ask the same children to explain in later trials 👉 compare if EIG- or PTS-users benefit more
